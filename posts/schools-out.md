# School's Out Forever

Earlier this month, I graduated from Georgia Tech with an M.S. in Music Technology.
It was a fun ride! I met talented folks, made cool sounds, and worked on the coolest projects.
I grew and learned a lot along the way, but throughout the program, I found myself using a lot of LLM-assisted coding tools.
Call it vibe coding, call it agentic workflows, call it slop. All of these descriptors hold some truth.

It is difficult to write about AI because part of me wants to impart a permanently relevant artefact - pure hubris, I know. 
With tools constantly changing and the economics of AI being increasingly hard to justify, it is not easy to classify if this is a long-winded trend or a more permanent fixture in software engineering. Personally, I'd lean towards the latter despite my skepticism.

I say this because when I started my graduate degree in 2024, AI coding was pretty much a hit or miss. 
It either worked or it didn't, and even when it did, it seemed to sacrificed everything: code style, personal understanding, novelty. All for what felt like laziness.

Today, after extensive use of Claude, Github Copilot, and a small dash of Codex, most of these issues can be mitigated through workflows both in and out of the box. 
Have a specific code style? Put it in a markdown file that Claude always reads. Need to review each line? Iterate slowly and comb through the diffs. Poke and prod as needed.

Novelty in AI coding comes from what it is you are trying to create. In Music Tech, that has and always will be the name of the game. 
For a lived example, there are not many guitar playing robots out there in the world. 
At least not when compared to how many B2B SaaS tools there are.

As the semesters passed, the way I used AI changed dramatically. 
But now I have the degree. No more deadlines. 
I have nothing concrete to do until September, which is when I start my very first full time job. 
So what do I do with my somewhat costly AI subscription? 
And *that*, my dear reader, is what I will try to explain here for you.

But first, how did I get **here** in the first place?

# The Act of Projecting

One required course in my program was a review of Music Technology through the lens of history and repetoire.
It involved a good bit of reading, discussion, and a mix of writing / projects as deliverables.
A key idea that stuck with me is Musicking, coined by Chris Small in his book titled such.
My classmates and I broadly interepreted musicking as a deliberate act, and that, regardless of whether the act resulted in what was subjectively considered music by its listeners or not, the act itself was of particular interest.

With this context in mind, a major throughline of my Masters program was submitting projects as main deliverables for a class. 
All but two of my classes were structured this way in one way or another.

Considering this is a technology program, coding was a huge part of projects, and thus, AI became a huge part of our degree.
What worked well from this was that I was able to transfer my skills in C++ to python or other languages very quickly.
I had a high level understanding of the architecture and stack of the project, threw it into an AI, and what came out of it often was good enough to work with to then finish the project.

This was nothing like my undergraduate experience. 
I recall submitting hand-written code to my programming professor with general distain, but it required I actually learn something. 
Granted, my undergrad degree was not focused on software. 
It was a mix of music, software and electrical engineering. 
I had mainly leaned into the software portion because it was quick and painless to iterate through.
